PROJECT 1Flagship

Climate-Proof Rainfall Design Standards for Coastal Cities

Drainage and flood defences in Lagos, Accra and Freetown are designed against a stationary climate that no longer exists. Non-stationary extreme value analysis, with global temperature as the covariate, turns projected rainfall extremes into the IDF curves and climate-adjustment factors engineers can drop straight into design codes.

30 monthsduration
$75kbudget
Lagos · Accra · Freetownstudy area
Met services · drainage authorities · engineering bodiespartners
  • Method core: GEV with location and scale varying with global mean temperature; shape held constant for stability, fully non-stationary form exploratory only.
  • Models: CORDEX-Africa ensemble behind a pre-registered validation gate, CMIP6 fallback ladder if too few models pass.
  • Deliverable that matters: updated IDF curves and design tables with uncertainty, co-designed with practising engineers from month 1.

PROJECT 2Evolved

From Maps to Warnings: Landslide Early Warning for Freetown

The 2017 Regent landslide killed over 1,100 people — and people cannot evacuate a susceptibility map. This project couples bias-corrected ensemble susceptibility mapping with rainfall intensity–duration thresholds into a prototype warning system, trialled in shadow mode through a full rainy season with Sierra Leone's National Disaster Management Agency.

42 monthsduration
$135kbudget
Freetown Peninsulastudy area
NDMA · SL Met Agency · Njala Universitypartners
  • Method core: spatial point-process sampling-bias correction proven on synthetic data first, then RF/XGBoost/MaxEnt ensemble with blocked cross-validation.
  • The evolution: the previous project treated warnings as an optional add-on; here the warning system is the deliverable and the map is an input.
  • Field programme: two dry-season campaigns, 80–100 power-analysis-sized validation sites, backup study area if access fails.

PROJECT 3New

Do AI Weather Models Work for West Africa?

AI models now run operationally at major forecast centres, but their headline scores are dominated by mid-latitude verification. Nobody has systematically tested them against the West African monsoon — the mesoscale-convection regime where they are most likely to fail while still scoring well globally. This project builds that benchmark, adds generative km-scale downscaling, and tests lightweight regional fine-tuning.

36 monthsduration
$110kbudget
West African monsoon regionstudy area
ACMAD · WASCAL · ECMWF (data) · met servicespartners
  • Deliverable that matters: WAM-Bench, an open monsoon-specific verification suite — the durable contribution in a fast-moving field.
  • Why it replaced classical downscaling: same regional need at 36 not 48 months, ~$110k not $140k, GPU credits instead of an institutional-HPC gate.
  • Honesty built in: pre-registered success criteria; a negative fine-tuning result is a publishable, operationally important answer.

PROJECT 4New

Humid Heat & Health in Freetown: an Impact-Based Warning System

Freetown appointed Africa's first Chief Heat Officer, yet the city has no heat-warning system and no measurements inside the settlements where humid-heat exposure is worst. A 50-sensor network measures wet-bulb conditions where people actually live, powers neighbourhood-scale forecasts, and feeds a co-designed three-tier alert system piloted through a full hot season.

30 monthsduration
$90kbudget
Freetownstudy area
City Council · Ministry of Health · SL Met Agencypartners
  • What was cut and why: satellite LST mapping (cloud-limited, not what humans feel) and hospital-data-dependent epidemiology (access risk) — the sensor network and health partnership were the salvageable core.
  • Method core: WBGT/wet-bulb metrics, ERA5-Land bias-corrected to each site, deliberately transparent downscaling the met agency can own.
  • Deliverable that matters: a working warning protocol handed to the City Council, with the network left running.

PROJECT 5New

When Hazards Collide: Compound Coastal Flood Risk

The worst urban floods happen when extreme rainfall lands on a city whose drains are already backed up by a high tide — a compound event. The dependence between rainfall extremes and coastal water levels has never been quantified for the Gulf of Guinea. Copula-based joint analysis, sea-level-rise scenarios and reduced-complexity flood modelling produce district-level compound-risk atlases for three megacity coastlines.

36 monthsduration
$105kbudget
Lagos · Accra · Freetownstudy area
Oceanography institutes · met agencies · city plannerspartners
  • Method core: bivariate copulas on joint rainfall/water-level catalogues, synoptic compositing for mechanism, LISFLOOD-FP-class inundation on FABDEM.
  • Portfolio link: consumes Project 1's non-stationary rainfall statistics so both flood drivers evolve consistently under warming.
  • Why it replaced the global emulator: a regional user and a live scientific frontier, instead of a global tool with no West African anchor.

What changed in the 2026 rethink

The previous portfolio was scientifically reviewed twice and patched twice. This redesign rebuilt it instead, keeping only what passed one test: does it change a decision in West Africa?

✅ Kept & sharpened

Extreme-precipitation EVT — the strongest project in every prior review — promoted to flagship with an engineering-first delivery plan.

🔁 Evolved

Landslide mapping → early warning system. Urban heat mapping → heat-health warnings. In both, the static map was demoted from deliverable to input.

🆕 Replaced

Classical CMIP6 downscaling (48 mo, $140k, HPC-gated) → AI-model benchmarking & generative downscaling. Global XAI emulator (no regional user) → compound coastal flood risk.

🗑️ Removed

Seven overlapping review, comparison and presentation documents — superseded by the proposals themselves and preserved in archive/.

Funding targets & open science

Every project commits to FAIR outputs — data on Zenodo (CC-BY-4.0), code on GitHub (MIT), preprints on EarthArXiv — and operational tools are hosted with regional institutions so they outlive the PhD.
