# Project 10 — Breathing the Harmattan: Verifying Dust Forecasts for Health Early Warning

**From unverified global dust models to health-advisory triggers for Sahelian and coastal West African cities**

| | |
|---|---|
| **Status** | Extension track |
| **Duration** | 30 months |
| **Budget** | $75,000 |
| **Study area** | Kano (Nigeria), Ouagadougou (Burkina Faso), Freetown (Sierra Leone, coastal contrast) |
| **Core methods** | Dust-forecast verification, low-cost PM sensor network with dust-specific calibration, health-advisory threshold co-design |
| **Primary partners** | WMO SDS-WAS Regional Center (Barcelona), national met services, ministries of health, WHO country offices |

---

## 1. Why this project

Every dry season the Harmattan carries Saharan dust across West Africa, driving spikes in respiratory and cardiovascular illness and historically shaping the meningitis belt's epidemics. The forecasting half of the problem is nominally solved — CAMS and the WMO SDS-WAS ensemble predict dust several days ahead, openly and operationally. But those forecasts are essentially unverified over West Africa at ground level (the models are tuned against satellite optical depth, not the surface concentrations lungs experience), and no West African country runs a dust-health advisory system that uses them. The missing pieces are exactly PhD-sized: ground-truth verification, a defensible link from forecast to exposure, and co-designed advisory triggers — the same playbook Project 4 applies to heat, aimed at a different hazard with different geography.

## 2. Research questions

1. How skilful are CAMS and SDS-WAS dust forecasts for *surface* PM concentrations in West African cities, at 1–5 day leads, across the Harmattan season?
2. Can low-cost PM sensors — with dust-specific calibration, since optical sensors systematically miscount coarse desert dust — provide adequate ground truth and real-time situational awareness at a cost national agencies can sustain?
3. What forecast-based advisory thresholds and channels would ministries of health actually adopt, and how would advisories perform over a pilot Harmattan season?

## 3. Data

- **Forecasts:** CAMS global atmospheric composition forecasts and reanalysis (EAC4); SDS-WAS multi-model ensemble.
- **Reference observations:** AERONET sun photometers (Ilorin, Banizoumbou and regional stations), MODIS/VIIRS aerosol optical depth.
- **New measurements:** 15 low-cost PM sensors (5 per city) plus one reference-grade instrument per city for collocation; two full Harmattan seasons of record.
- **Health context:** WHO air-quality guideline bands; existing meningitis-belt surveillance literature; aggregated syndromic indicators only where a ministry agreement exists (enrichment, never a dependency — the Project 4 rule).

## 4. Methodology

**WP1 — Sensor network and calibration (months 1–12).** Deploy sensors before the first Harmattan; develop dust-specific calibration against collocated reference instruments — coarse-mode undercounting by optical sensors is a known, publishable methods problem, and solving it credibly is a contribution in itself, transferable across the Sahel.

**WP2 — Forecast verification (months 6–20).** Verify CAMS/SDS-WAS surface-concentration and optical-depth forecasts against the sensor network, AERONET and satellite AOD over two seasons: skill by lead time, event onset/cessation timing, and severity-class discrimination (the categories advisories need). Deliverable: the first ground-truthed verification of operational dust forecasts for West African cities.

**WP3 — Advisory design (months 14–24).** With ministries of health and met services, translate WHO guideline bands and local baselines into a three-tier advisory (sensitive-groups / general / severe) triggered by forecast + observed thresholds; define channels (radio, health-facility bulletins, schools) per city.

**WP4 — Pilot season and evaluation (months 22–30).** Run advisories through one Harmattan season in at least two cities; evaluate trigger accuracy against observations and advisory reach/comprehension through health-facility and community surveys. Hand the running system to the met service–health ministry pair with documentation and training.

## 5. Risks and decision gates

| Risk | Likelihood | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| Low-cost sensors uncorrectable for coarse dust | Moderate | Reference collocation from day one; fallback to AERONET+satellite verification (WP2 survives) |
| Forecast skill turns out poor | Moderate | A rigorous negative result redirects agencies away from false confidence — publishable and useful |
| Three-country coordination overhead | Moderate–high | **Month-10 gate:** drop to two cities (keep Kano + Freetown for the Sahel–coast contrast) |
| Health-ministry engagement stalls | Moderate | Advisories default to WHO physiological bands; ministry input enriches, never gates |

**Minimum viable thesis:** calibrated two-season dust record plus the forecast verification — 2 papers, no pilot required.

## 6. Outcomes

- Peer-reviewed papers (2–3): dust-specific low-cost sensor calibration; forecast verification for West African cities; pilot advisory evaluation.
- **Open two-season, three-city surface dust dataset** — the first of its kind for the region.
- A running dust-health advisory pilot owned by national agencies, with a transferable design.

## 7. Fit in the portfolio

Applies Project 4's measure→predict→threshold→pilot playbook to the portfolio's first air-quality hazard, extends geographic reach into the Sahel, and shares low-cost-sensor procurement, calibration practice and health-sector relationships with the heat-health project.
