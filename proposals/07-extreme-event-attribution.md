# Project 7 — Whose Weather? Rapid Extreme-Event Attribution Capability for West Africa

**Adapting, validating and operationalising attribution science for the world's most under-attributed region**

| | |
|---|---|
| **Status** | Extension track |
| **Duration** | 36 months |
| **Budget** | $80,000 |
| **Study area** | West Africa (Sahelian and Guinean zones); 3–4 event case studies |
| **Core methods** | Probabilistic event attribution, covariate-based non-stationary statistics, model validation for extremes, rapid-response protocol design |
| **Primary partners** | World Weather Attribution network, ACMAD, national met services, Red Cross Red Crescent Climate Centre |

---

## 1. Why this project

When floods hit Germany or heatwaves hit North America, attribution scientists publish within weeks how much climate change altered the odds. When the 2022 floods displaced 1.4 million people in Nigeria, the region waited months for a single study — and most West African disasters are never attributed at all. The reasons are structural: sparse observations, climate models with known monsoon biases, and almost no regional attribution capacity. Meanwhile the stakes have risen sharply: the UN Loss and Damage Fund is operational, and an attribution statement is now an input to finance claims, not just a headline.

The science question is genuinely open — *which attribution methods survive West African data and model limitations?* — and the capability question is genuinely practical: a validated, pre-built regional pipeline that turns a months-long study into a weeks-long one.

## 2. Research questions

1. Which combinations of observational products and model ensembles pass fitness-for-purpose tests for attributing West African extreme rainfall and heat — and where do standard attribution protocols break?
2. What do rigorous attributions of 3–4 major recent events (e.g., the 2022 Niger-basin floods, recent Sahel flood seasons, a Guinean-coast extreme-rain event, a humid-heat event) conclude, with honest uncertainty?
3. Can a pre-validated rapid-attribution protocol for the region deliver a defensible result within 4–6 weeks of an event, and how should its outputs be framed for loss-and-damage and adaptation decisions?

## 3. Data

- **Observations:** CHIRPS, TAMSAT, gauge records via met services, ERA5 (with documented caveats as a quasi-observation); GHCN for temperature.
- **Models:** CMIP6 (historical + hist-nat where available), CORDEX-Africa, HighResMIP; large ensembles where accessible.
- **Statistical backbone:** covariate-based non-stationary GEV/GPD scaling with GMST — directly importing Project 1's fitted framework.
- **Event catalogue:** compiled from EM-DAT, national disaster agencies and media archives, with impact metadata.

## 4. Methodology

**WP1 — Fitness-for-purpose (months 1–12).** Systematically evaluate observation products and model ensembles against the specific statistics attribution needs (tail behaviour, trend representation, monsoon process realism), not generic climatology. Deliverable: a documented, reusable model/observation screening for West African attribution — the missing foundation every future study needs.

**WP2 — Retrospective case studies (months 8–26).** Full probabilistic attributions of 3–4 events spanning hazard types and climate zones, using the multi-method standard (observational scaling + validated model ensembles), reporting probability ratios and intensity changes with full uncertainty. Where methods disagree or evidence is inconclusive, saying so explicitly is a core result — over-claiming is the field's biggest reputational risk.

**WP3 — Rapid protocol (months 20–32).** Convert WP1+WP2 into a pre-computed regional pipeline (screened ensembles cached, code tested, thresholds pre-registered) and run one live trial: when a qualifying event occurs, execute the protocol end-to-end with ACMAD and WWA collaborators, targeting a public statement in 4–6 weeks.

**WP4 — Decision interface (months 26–36).** With the Climate Centre and legal/policy collaborators, analyse how attribution statements are actually used — loss-and-damage submissions, national adaptation plans, public communication — and produce framing guidance: what an attribution number can and cannot support.

## 5. Risks and decision gates

| Risk | Likelihood | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| Most models fail extreme-rainfall validation | Moderate–high | Observational-scaling attribution (Project 1 framework) carries the analysis; model results become conditional evidence |
| No qualifying live event during WP3 | Low | Sadly unlikely; fallback is a timed "fire-drill" re-run of a past event under rapid-response conditions |
| hist-nat availability limits ensemble size | Moderate | GMST-covariate methods need no counterfactual runs; model methods supplement |
| Results politicised or over-interpreted | Moderate | WP4 framing guidance; WWA communication standards; uncertainty always headline-level |

**Minimum viable thesis:** fitness-for-purpose screening plus two completed case-study attributions — 2 papers.

## 6. Outcomes

- Peer-reviewed papers (2–3): the regional fitness-for-purpose framework; case-study attributions; the rapid-protocol trial.
- **An operational rapid-attribution pipeline** held at ACMAD, with trained regional collaborators.
- Public attribution statements for 3–4 major West African events — likely the first for most of them.
- Framing guidance for loss-and-damage applications of attribution results.

## 7. Fit in the portfolio

Reuses Project 1's non-stationary statistical machinery on a new question; gives every hazard project in the portfolio (floods, heat) a "was this climate change?" answer path; and is the portfolio's most direct link to international climate finance.
